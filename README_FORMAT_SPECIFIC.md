**The Best New Artist "curse" is actually an ALBUMS curse, not a singles curse.**

### Statistical Evidence (41 Artists, 1958-2010)

| Metric | Hot 100 (Singles) | Billboard 200 (Albums) |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Curse Rate** | 51.5% | 75.7% |
| **P-value** | 0.2293 (NOT significant) | **0.0002 (highly significant)** |
| **Average Decline** | +5.3 positions | +23.1 positions |
| **Interpretation** | No statistical curse | **Strong statistical curse** |

### What This Means

**Winners CAN sustain singles success:**
- Only 52% show decline (essentially random)
- Not statistically significant (p > 0.05)
- Examples: The Beatles (-19 singles positions), Tracy Chapman (-30), Robert Goulet (-49)

**Winners CANNOT sustain albums success:**
- 76% show decline (3 out of 4 artists)
- Extremely significant (p < 0.001)
- Average decline 4x worse than singles
- Examples: Bobbie Gentry (+120), LeAnn Rimes (+68), Lauryn Hill (+61)

### Pattern Breakdown (32 artists with both singles & albums data)

| Pattern | Percentage | Interpretation |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| **Albums cursed, Singles escaped** | 47% | Can make hit singles, can't replicate album success |
| **Both cursed** | 28% | Comprehensive decline |
| **Singles cursed, Albums escaped** | 22% | Album artists who don't rely on singles |
| **Both escaped** | 3% | Only Bruce Hornsby & The Range |

### Why This Matters

**Previous understanding:** "BNA winners are cursed commercially"

**Data-driven reality:** "BNA winners struggle with sophomore albums but can maintain singles chart presence. The curse is format-specific, not career-wide."

**Hypothesis:** Debut albums represent years of accumulated best material. Singles require one great song. Replicating a successful album is structurally harder than producing hit singles.
